Driver Fatigue Detection System Report

Importance: Accidents caused by driver fatigue constitute a significant proportion of traffic accidents, leading to the loss of thousands of lives, millions of hours in time, and billions of dollars in financial losses each year, solely in the US. This makes it a highly crucial issue.

Different Solutions

Various methods can minimize this problem, such as machine learning models based on the car's path, monitoring the driver's pulse, or tracking body symptoms using techniques like EEG. Among these, a system employing Computer Vision for blink, yawn detection, and head estimation is likely the most useful and effective, as it directly monitors the driver's physical condition. It is highly cost-efficient since it only requires a Raspberry Pi or Arduino-like system, a camera, and an alarm.

Methodology

Step 1: Detection of Person, Face, Eyes, and Mouth

To enhance the mentioned system, the first step would be to detect a person using the camera, followed by identifying the person's face and subsequently detecting their eyes and mouth. For this purpose, there is an excellent machine learning library called dlib, written in C. With dlib, it is possible to access real-time images of the left eye, right eye, and mouth, facilitating the development of the desired features.

Step 2: Determination of Drowsiness

At this point, since we have images of the person's eyes and mouth, efforts should be focused on how to determine whether the person is drowsy from these images. The methods used here are as follows: 2.1) Blink detection - the condition of the eyelids being closed for a certain period 2.2) Yawn detection - the condition of the mouth being open for an extended period 2.3) Head pose detection - measuring the tilt of the head, which may indicate drowsiness if it is tilted to the sides 2.4) Excessive yawning within a certain time frame, 2.5) Tracking the blink rate over a certain period since the blink rate decreases in sleepy individuals

Step 3: Measuring the Level of Fatigue

At this point, the next step should be measuring the level of fatigue in the individual. Our system has four status levels: 3.1) The driver is perfectly alert. 3.2) The driver shows signs of fatigue, having blinked less than the normal rate or yawned more than usual within a certain period. 3.3) The driver is in a drowsy state, having yawned for an extended period or with eyes closed for a certain duration. 3.4) The driver is dozing off, keeping their eyes closed for an extended period or tilting their head to the sides. In such cases, an alarm will be triggered.

Step 4: Determining Closed Eyes, Open Mouth, and Head Tilt

The most crucial part is how to determine if the eyes are closed, the mouth is open, and the head is tilted to the sides. There are two different methods for this: 4.1) The first method involves creating a model using Transfer Learning and using this model to make decisions. Kaggle datasets were utilized for this purpose, and MobileNet was chosen as a lightweight Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). This allowed the trained machine learning model to categorize in real-time using the camera, detecting whether the eyes are closed, the mouth is open, or the head is tilted. 4.2) Additionally, a model was developed that treats every significant point on the face (a total of 68 points) as landmarks. This model mathematically measures the distance between the eyes and lips. According to this model, surpassing a certain threshold indicates blinking or yawning, and further details can be accessed from the report. Finally, the head pose is obtained by calculating the angle between the eyes' centers, using these two points as a reference. In our system, we aimed to enhance sensitivity by simultaneously using these two models, thereby increasing the recall value.
